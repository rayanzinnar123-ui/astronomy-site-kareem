Scientific Analysis

A. Relationship between the illuminated shape of the Moon and the position of the Sun

A clear and consistent relationship was observed between the illuminated shape of the Moon and the relative position of the Sun. In all observations, the illuminated portion of the Moon was always oriented toward the direction of the Sun in the sky. In other words, the bright side of the Moon effectively “points” directly at the Sun. When the Sun was located to the left of the Moon, the illuminated portion appeared on the left side; when the Sun was to the right, the illumination shifted accordingly. This pattern was consistently verified through my sketches, where arrows drawn from the Moon toward the Sun aligned with the direction of the illuminated edge.

This relationship held across multiple observations taken on different dates and times, such as February 11 (≈60°), February 12 (≈50°), and February 13 (≈35°), where the illuminated portion became progressively smaller (see respective sketches and images in the calendar section) while still maintaining its orientation toward the Sun. Therefore, the observed shape of the Moon is directly determined by its angular position relative to the Sun, and the direction of illumination always indicates the Sun’s position.

B. How can we tell that the Moon shines by reflected sunlight?

From our observations, it has become clear that the Moon shines by reflected sunlight because the illuminated portion of the Moon always aligns with the position of the Sun. In every case, the bright side of the Moon was oriented toward the Sun, as confirmed by our sketches where arrows drawn toward the Sun matched the direction of illumination.

If the Moon was producing its own light, the illuminated portion would not consistently depend on the Sun’s position and would appear independent of its direction. However, this was never observed. Instead, the illumination always depended on the relative position between the Moon and the Sun (measured angular distance across the straightest possible line). For example, during the first observed waning crescent phase (February 11 to February 13), the illuminated portion decreased as the Moon moved closer to the Sun in the sky (as the angular distance decreased). However, as the angular distance increased as time passed, i.e. the Moon and the Sun grew further apart we noticed that the illuminated portion of the moon increased in size as well (eg, February 27 [140°]) and then continued to decrease again as the angular distance shrunk (March 10 to March 15).

Therefore, the only logical explanation is that the Moon reflects sunlight, and the observed phases are a result of the changing angle between the Sun, the Moon, and the observer.

C. Why is it valid that observations were taken from different locations?

One important point to address is why it is valid that our observations were taken from different locations (Beirut and Baalbek). At first glance, this might seem like it would affect the angular measurements between the Moon and the Sun, especially since we relied on hand-based estimations such as fists and fingers. However, the Moon–Sun angular separation is an astronomical quantity that depends on their relative positions in the sky, not on the observer’s exact position on Earth, since these distances are negligible compared to celestial scales.

Additionally, due to the Moon’s synchronous rotation (tidal locking), observers on Earth see essentially the same face of the Moon at any given time, further reinforcing the consistency of observations across locations. While the orientation of the sky may vary slightly depending on the observer’s position, the angular separation itself remains effectively unchanged.

This is supported by the consistency in recorded data, where a clear decreasing trend in angular separation was observed during the waning crescent phase (from about 60° on February 11 to about 35° on February 13), and an increasing trend after the new moon (reaching about 140° on February 27). Any minor discrepancies are more reasonably attributed to the limitations of hand-based estimation and photographic effects rather than differences in observation location.

D. How can we tell that the Sun is much farther away than the Moon?

From recorded observations, we can conclude that the Sun is much farther away than the Moon because the direction of illumination is always consistent and depends only on the relative position of the Sun, that is, the angular distance between the Sun and the Moon. In every case, the illuminated portion of the Moon pointed directly towards the Sun, regardless of the time or location of observation.

This indicates that the light reaching the Moon behaves as if it is coming from a single, fixed direction. Physically, light from a nearby source spreads out in different directions (diverges), while light from a very distant source appears almost parallel. The fact that the illumination of the Moon is always perfectly aligned with the Sun means that the sunlight reaching the Moon must be nearly parallel.

If the Sun were close to the Moon, the light rays would not be parallel, and the direction of illumination would vary depending on the observer’s position on Earth. However, this was never observed; the alignment remained consistent in all cases. Therefore, the only logical conclusion is that the Sun is extremely far away compared to the Moon, which is why its light reaches the Moon in nearly parallel rays and produces a consistent illumination pattern.

Understanding the Diagram

This diagram shows a top-down view of the Earth-Moon-Sun system (not to scale). Sunlight arrives from the right side of the diagram. As the Moon orbits Earth, different portions of its illuminated hemisphere become visible from Earth, creating the lunar phases we observe.

  • New Moon (0°): Moon between Earth and Sun; dark side faces Earth
  • First Quarter (90°): Moon at right angle; right half illuminated
  • Full Moon (180°): Earth between Moon and Sun; fully illuminated
  • Last Quarter (270°): Moon at left angle; left half illuminated

Conclusion

This observational study of the Moon over a multi-week period has provided direct evidence supporting fundamental principles of lunar astronomy. Through systematic observation, documentation, and analysis, several key conclusions emerge regarding the nature of moonlight and the geometry of the Earth-Moon-Sun system.

Key Findings

  • ◐ The lunar phase cycle follows a predictable ~29.5-day period, progressing through new moon, waxing crescent, first quarter, waxing gibbous, full moon, waning gibbous, last quarter, and waning crescent phases.
  • ☀ The illuminated portion of the Moon consistently faces the Sun's position, demonstrating that moonlight is reflected sunlight rather than self-generated luminescence.
  • ◑ The angular separation between the Moon and Sun directly correlates with the observed phase, with larger separations corresponding to greater illuminated fractions.
  • ⊕ The geometry of observed phases confirms that the Sun must be significantly more distant than the Moon, as evidenced by the phase cycle only being possible with a distant, external light source.

These observations reinforce the heliocentric model of our solar system and demonstrate how careful, systematic observation can reveal fundamental truths about celestial mechanics. The Moon Journal serves as both a record of scientific inquiry and evidence of the predictable, geometric nature of lunar phenomena.
